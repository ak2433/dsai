## Course Description

This course offers a rigorous, interdisciplinary examination of terrorism from antiquity to the present day. Students will analyze the ideological, philosophical, political, psychological, and technological dimensions of terrorism across cultures and centuries. The course moves beyond surface-level event recounting to interrogate the *why* behind political violence — exploring how grievances are radicalized into action, how states respond, and how societies are reshaped by terror. Students will engage with primary sources, case studies, philosophical texts, and policy documents. By the end of the semester, students will possess the analytical tools to critically evaluate terrorism as a historical phenomenon and contemporary threat.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

1. Define and critically analyze competing definitions of "terrorism" and "political violence"
2. Trace the historical evolution of terrorist tactics, ideologies, and organizations from antiquity to the 21st century
3. Evaluate the philosophical and ethical debates surrounding the use of political violence
4. Analyze the role of technology — from gunpowder to the internet — in shaping the methods and reach of terrorism
5. Compare and contrast religious, nationalist, anarchist, Marxist, and far-right/far-left terrorist movements
6. Assess state responses to terrorism, including counterterrorism strategy, civil liberties trade-offs, and the ethics of torture
7. Construct well-supported written arguments using primary and secondary sources
8. Engage respectfully with sensitive, morally complex material

## WEEK 1: Defining the Undefinable

### Monday — Introduction: Why Study Terrorism?

**Topics:**
- Course overview, expectations, and ground rules
- Why definitions matter: "One man's terrorist is another man's freedom fighter"
- The academic study of terrorism: challenges, biases, and blind spots
- Overview of major terrorist waves (Rapoport's Four Waves theory)

**Lecture Focus:**
David Rapoport's seminal "Four Waves" framework posits that modern terrorism has moved through four distinct waves: the Anarchist Wave (1880s–1920s), the Anti-Colonial Wave (1920s–1960s), the New Left Wave (1960s–1990s), and the Religious Wave (1979–present). This framework provides a structural backbone for the entire course.

**Required Reading:**
- ★ Rapoport, D.C. (2004). "The Four Waves of Modern Terrorism." In *Attacking Terrorism*, ed. Cronin & Ludes. Ch. 2 *(Course Reader)*

### Wednesday — The Problem of Definition

**Topics:**

- The social construction of terrorism: who gets labeled?

**Key Conceptual Distinctions:**
- **Terrorism:** Deliberate targeting of civilians for political ends
- **Guerrilla warfare:** Military tactics against state forces by non-state actors
- **State terrorism:** State use of terror against its own population
- **Narco-terrorism:** Intersection of drug trafficking and political violence

**Required Reading:**

### Friday — Philosophical Frameworks for Political Violence

**Topics:**

- Consequentialist defenses of terrorism (end justifies the means)
- Deontological objections (Kant, absolute prohibition on targeting innocents)
- The "Ticking Time Bomb" scenario: a philosophical stress test

**Deep Dive — Just War Theory:**
St. Augustine and St. Thomas Aquinas established conditions for legitimate war: just cause, right intention, proper authority, last resort, probability of success, and proportionality. Students will examine how terrorist organizations *appropriate* just war language to legitimize violence, and where that appropriation fails philosophically.

**Required Reading:**

**Discussion Question:**

**Lecture Focus — Moghaddam's Staircase Model:**
Fathali Moghaddam's six-floor "staircase to terrorism" describes psychological progression: perceived injustice → displaced aggression → moral engagement with terrorist group → categorical thinking → willingness to use violence → "terrorist acts." Most people remain on lower floors; only a tiny fraction reach the top.

## WEEK 4: Ancient and Medieval Terror

### Monday — Terror in the Ancient World

**Topics:**
- Assyrian state terror as military strategy (900–600 BCE)
- Roman decimation and collective punishment
- The Sicarii (Jewish Zealots, 1st century CE): the first recorded terrorist group?
- Alexander the Great and the strategic use of mass atrocity

**Lecture Focus — The Sicarii:**
The Sicarii (Latin: *sicarius*, "dagger-man") were a splinter faction of Jewish Zealots active in Judea during the first century CE. They conducted assassinations of Roman collaborators in crowded public spaces, then disappeared into the crowd — the first recorded use of what we would now call urban guerrilla tactics. Their goal was to incite Roman reprisals, thereby radicalizing the Jewish population.

**Required Reading:**

**Lecture Focus:**
The Nizari Ismailis, known in Western sources as "the Assassins," operated for nearly two centuries as a sophisticated network of politically motivated killers. Unlike armies, they deployed single operatives to kill high-value targets — sultans, caliphs, Crusader leaders — in ways designed to maximize psychological impact. They were, in effect, the first recorded non-state group to systematically weaponize targeted assassination.

## WEEK 5: The Anarchist Wave (1880s–1920s)

### Monday — Anarchist Ideology and the "Propaganda of the Deed"

**Topics:**
- Origins of anarchism: Proudhon, Bakunin, Kropotkin
- "Propaganda of the Deed": Mikhail Bakunin and Carlo Pisacane
- The logic of spectacular violence: why assassinate, not organize?
- Nihilism and the philosophy of destruction as creation

**Deep Dive — Propaganda of the Deed:**
In 1848, Italian revolutionary Carlo Pisacane argued that "the propaganda of the idea is a chimera. Ideas result from deeds, not the latter from the former." This concept — that a dramatic violent act communicates more than a thousand pamphlets — became the philosophical engine of anarchist terrorism. Bakunin refined it: destruction itself was a creative act that would awaken the oppressed masses.

**Required Reading:**
- ★ Bakunin, M. — "The Reaction in Germany" and "Letters to a Frenchman" (excerpts) *(Course Reader)*
- Kropotkin, P. — "The Spirit of Revolt" (1880) *(Course Reader)*

### Wednesday — Political Assassinations of the Gilded Age

**Topics:**
- The assassination of Tsar Alexander II (1881) — Narodnaya Volya

- Assassination of Empress Elisabeth of Austria (1898)
- The Attentäter: profiles of individual anarchist assassins
- Technology: the revolver and dynamite as democratizers of violence

**Lecture Focus — Narodnaya Volya ("The People's Will"):**
The Russian revolutionary organization Narodnaya Volya successfully assassinated Tsar Alexander II in St. Petersburg in 1881 after multiple failed attempts. The group's sophisticated structure — including an Executive Committee, safe houses, and coded communication — anticipated many features of modern terrorist organizations. Paradoxically, Alexander II had been in the process of granting a limited constitution; his death ended that project and brought brutal reaction under Alexander III.

**Required Reading:**

**Lecture Focus — Michael Collins and the Intelligence War:**
Michael Collins pioneered the systematic infiltration and dismantling of British intelligence networks. By assassinating the "Cairo Gang" (British intelligence agents) on Bloody Sunday (November 21, 1920), Collins left the British effectively blind in Ireland. His methods — small cells, deniability, civilian cover — became a template for insurgencies worldwide, most notably studied by Yasser Arafat and Mao Zedong.

**Deep Dive — Frantz Fanon:**
In *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961), Martinique-born psychiatrist Frantz Fanon argued that colonized peoples suffer a psychic wound from their dehumanization, and that violence against the colonizer is not merely politically necessary — it is psychologically *therapeutic*, restoring the colonized person's humanity. Jean-Paul Sartre's inflammatory preface endorsed this view. Few texts have been more influential — and more controversial — in justifying anti-colonial violence.

**Deep Dive — Sayyid Qutb:**
Egyptian intellectual Sayyid Qutb (1906–1966) is arguably the most consequential theorist of modern Islamic terrorism. After being imprisoned and tortured by Nasser's government, he wrote *Milestones* (1964), arguing that all existing Muslim societies had reverted to *jahiliyyah* (pre-Islamic paganism) and that a vanguard of true believers must wage violent *jihad* to restore God's sovereignty. His execution by Nasser in 1966 made him a martyr whose ideas spread globally. Bin Laden, Zawahiri, and virtually all al-Qaeda leadership cite Qutb as foundational.

**Technology Focus — Stuxnet:**
Stuxnet (discovered 2010, likely deployed 2007) was a 500KB computer worm jointly developed by the U.S. and Israel to sabotage Iran's Natanz uranium enrichment facility. **Lecture Focus — How Do Terrorist Campaigns End?**
A landmark RAND study (Jones & Libicki, 2008) examined 268 terrorist groups active since 1968. Results: 43% ended through policing/intelligence (not military force), 40% through political process/negotiation, 10% through military defeat, 7% through achievement of goals. Only 7% were defeated by military means alone. The implications for counterterrorism policy are profound: mass violence suppression rarely works; patient intelligence and political legitimacy usually do.

## Appendix D: A Note on Objectivity and Analytical Distance

Terrorism is one of the most morally loaded topics in the contemporary world. Students and instructors alike carry personal histories, political commitments, and emotional reactions into the classroom. This course asks you to do something difficult: to understand the internal logic of movements you may find morally repugnant.

Understanding is not endorsement. To explain why someone planted a bomb is not to excuse the act. To reconstruct al-Qaeda's strategic reasoning is not to validate it. To read Fanon with seriousness is not to endorse political violence. The intellectual obligation to understand — fully, rigorously, charitably — is what distinguishes a scholar from a pundit.

The greatest insight this course can give you is this: terrorism almost always *makes sense* from the inside. It emerges from comprehensible grievances, coherent (if often flawed) ideologies, and recognizable human psychology. This is not comforting. It is, however, essential. You cannot dismantle what you refuse to understand.
